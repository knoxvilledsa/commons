About this prototype / How it is hosted

Under the hood, in plain terms

How this site is hosted and run

No server to babysit, nothing to patch, about 0 USD a month. Here is what that actually means, told the way you would explain it to a neighbor at a meeting.

The big idea, in one picture

Think about the website the chapter has now as an apartment the chapter rents and has to maintain itself. The plumbing, the locks, the wiring, every repair, all of it is the chapter’s problem. In website terms that “plumbing” is a computer running somewhere (a server), a filing cabinet of content (a database), and a pile of add-ons (the WordPress plugins). When nobody has time to keep up the repairs, things leak, locks fail, and the place falls into disrepair. That is the burden of any site with moving parts that a volunteer chapter has to keep patched.

The new site is more like a printed flyer pinned to a public board that the city keeps up. You hand over finished pages, and a service simply keeps them on the wall for everyone to read. There is no plumbing inside to spring a leak, no lock to pick, because nothing is actually “running” behind it. It is just the finished pages, sitting there, handed to a service whose whole job is to keep them online and fast.

That one shift, from a machine you have to tend to a finished thing you hand off, is why the new site costs almost nothing, almost never breaks, and needs no technical person on call.

What is in the box

The three pieces

The whole setup is just three free or nearly free services, each with one clear job. None of them is something the chapter has to run or repair.

The pages · Astro

The pages

Astro is a static site builder, which means it bakes every page ahead of time, like printing the flyers before the meeting instead of writing them on the spot. By the time a visitor arrives there is nothing left to compute, so the site loads fast and there is almost nothing that can break.

See it build › The editor · Sanity

The editor

Sanity (free for a chapter this size) is the CMS, which is just a fancy word for the place you type the content. Volunteers sign in with their Gmail and fill in a friendly online form to write a post or add an event. No code, no setup, nothing scary.

Try the form › The host · Cloudflare

The host

Cloudflare Pages is a global service that keeps the finished pages online, adds the little padlock for a secure connection (HTTPS) automatically, and shrugs off attacks. It works as a CDN, meaning it keeps copies of the pages close to each visitor so they load fast everywhere.

Volunteers type in Sanity, Astro bakes the pages, and Cloudflare keeps them on the wall for the world to read.

Start to finish

The volunteer publishing flow

From the moment someone clicks Publish to the moment a neighbor can read it, the whole trip takes about a minute, and the volunteer does not have to think about a single step past the first one.

1

Fill the form

A volunteer signs in with Gmail, writes the post or event in the online form, and clicks Publish. That is the only human step.

2

The site rebuilds

That click quietly tells the site to bake itself again, the way a print shop runs a fresh batch of flyers with the new copy added.

3

Pages go out

The finished pages are copied out to the host’s computers around the world, so they sit close to whoever opens them.

4

It is live

About a minute later the post is online for everyone, with the secure padlock already in place. No one had to touch a server.

It keeps itself current

The reason the old site looked abandoned was simple: keeping it up to date was a chore one person had to remember, and when they got busy, it froze. The new site is built so that does not happen. Every recurring date keeps itself current on its own, not just the monthly General Meeting but the working group meetings, the reading group, the regular mutual aid distributions, all of them. The site works out the next occurrence from each schedule (the General Meeting on the fourth Monday, the transit group on the second Monday, the reading group on the third Thursday, and so on) instead of waiting for a volunteer to retype them every month. The dates in this very prototype already work that way, which is why they read correctly the day you opened it and will still be correct months from now, with nobody touching them. Anything a volunteer posts on top of that is live in about a minute. And when a newcomer signs up, they get a friendly welcome on their own, with the next meeting and how to plug in.

So the site stays as alive as the chapter is, without a webmaster babysitting it, and none of it rests on one person. If the volunteer who set it up moves on, the next one keeps it running from a short written guide. That is the whole point: the work that used to quietly rot the old site is handled for you now.

Why this is safer

There is no server sitting online waiting to be broken into, no database to spill, and no plugins to keep patched. The entire category of attacks that targets database-backed sites simply does not apply here, because none of those moving parts exist anymore. The host takes care of security, the padlock, and sudden traffic spikes (say, the night a campaign goes viral), so the chapter never has to.

Why it is nearly free

The free tiers of these services cover a chapter our size with room to spare, because keeping finished pages on a wall costs the host almost nothing. The only standing cost is the domain name, the chapter’s web address, which runs about 12 to 15 USD a year. Everything else is 0 USD a month.

Who could break it

Editors only ever touch the content: the words, the photos, the event dates. The layout is code that lives somewhere volunteers never see and never need to. So a typo in a post can never take the site down, and there is no “wrong button” that breaks the design.

Fair questions

The questions members ask

Short, plain answers to the things that come up whenever the chapter talks about changing how the site runs.

Do we need someone technical to run it day to day?

No. Day to day, running the site means writing posts and adding events, and that is a form anyone who can use email can fill out. There is nothing to maintain, patch, or restart between posts.

What if Cloudflare or Sanity disappears?

The site is just files kept in the chapter’s own repository (its own copy of everything). If a service ever changed its terms or shut down, those same files could be handed to another host, the same way you could pin your flyer to a different public board.

What about our .org domain?

The chapter keeps the domain name and simply points it at the new host, the way you would forward your mail to a new address. The old site stays up the whole time and the switch only happens once the new one is ready.

Can we still use Action Network?

Yes. The RSVP and sign-up forms connect straight to Action Network, just as they do now, so the chapter’s event and contact lists keep working exactly the same.

Is our content locked in?

No. Everything the chapter writes can be exported out at any time. The content belongs to the chapter, not to any one service.

The short version

A site a volunteer can run

No server, no database, no plugins to patch. If you can use Gmail, you can publish, and the whole thing costs about a dollar a month. See why the chapter is making the switch.
