For everyone in Knoxville

Know Your Rights and Community Defense

You have rights, no matter your immigration status, no matter who is asking. This page explains what those rights are in Knoxville and East Tennessee, in plain language you can use in a stressful moment. Read it before you need it. Save the numbers. Print the wallet card. And plug in with the chapter so none of us faces this alone.

This page is legal information, not legal advice. It is current as of 2026 and describes the law as best we understand it, but laws change and every situation is different. Some parts of this guide describe rights that are being actively fought over in court right now, and we note those clearly. Nothing here creates an attorney-client relationship. For advice about your specific situation, contact one of the organizations named on this page, especially TIRRC, Legal Aid of East Tennessee, or Catholic Charities of East Tennessee. Rights That Apply to Everyone

These rights come from the U.S. Constitution and apply to every person in the United States, regardless of immigration status or documentation. They are not automatic, though. You usually have to speak up to use them. Merely staying quiet or standing by can waive your protection, so say the words out loud.

Your rights

  • You have the right to remain silent. You do not have to answer questions about where you were born, how you entered the United States, or your immigration status. To use this right you generally must say you are using it: 'I am exercising my right to remain silent.'
  • You have the right to refuse consent to a search of your body, your car, your belongings, or your home. Say: 'I do not consent to a search.' Refusing does not make you look guilty, and saying it out loud, ideally in front of witnesses or a camera, helps your lawyer challenge any search later.
  • You have the right to speak with a lawyer before answering questions or signing anything. In criminal cases you have the right to a court-appointed lawyer. In immigration cases there is no free government lawyer, so the right is to consult one you find or a nonprofit, not to have one provided.
  • These rights are not absolute. They can be narrower at the border and at ports of entry, and federal agents claim expanded stop authority within 100 miles of a border or coastline. Knoxville is well outside that zone, so that claimed authority does not apply here. Certain narrow disclosure duties also apply, such as a driver showing a license.
  • You should never lie to a federal agent, and you should never falsely claim to be a U.S. citizen. Lying can be a separate crime, and a false claim to citizenship can permanently bar you from ever getting legal status, with almost no exceptions.
  • A false claim to citizenship can happen on paper, not just out loud. This includes checking the 'citizen' box on a job form (I-9), a voter registration, or a loan application.
Say this out loud

“I am exercising my right to remain silent.”

“I do not consent to a search.”

“I want to speak with a lawyer.”

“Am I free to go?”

Do

  • Say your rights out loud, clearly and calmly.
  • Keep your hands visible and stay calm in any encounter.
  • Write down what happened as soon as you are safe: names, badge numbers, agency, time, and witnesses.

Do not

  • Do not stay silent without saying you are invoking the right, because silence alone may not protect you.
  • Do not physically resist, even if you believe your rights are being violated. Contest it later, in court, with a lawyer.
  • Do not sign anything you do not fully understand or that a lawyer has not reviewed.
Tennessee note

These constitutional protections apply statewide, but Tennessee has specific rules for traffic stops, for identifying yourself, and for recording police that are covered in the sections below. Read those before you act.

If ICE Comes to Your Home or Approaches You

ICE agents cannot lawfully enter your home without your consent, a warrant signed by a judge, or a true emergency such as chasing someone inside. Most ICE warrants are NOT signed by a judge. Knowing the difference, and keeping your door closed, is the single most protective thing you can do.

Your rights

  • You do not have to open your door. You can speak to agents through a closed door. Say: 'I do not consent to you entering my home.'
  • Anyone at the door may say they are 'police' or are investigating a crime to get you to open it. Ask through the closed door which agency they are from, and keep the door closed either way.
  • Only a judicial warrant lets agents enter without your consent. A judicial warrant names a court, such as a United States District Court or a state court, and is signed by a judge or magistrate. An ICE administrative warrant (Form I-200 or I-205) says Department of Homeland Security and is signed by an ICE officer, not a judge. It does NOT authorize entry into your home.
  • Ask agents to slide any warrant under the door or hold it to a window so you can read it without opening the door. Even cracking the door open can be treated as consent to enter, so keep it fully closed.
  • The prevailing view among immigrant-rights legal organizations (ILRC, NILC, and the ACLU) is that an ICE administrative warrant does not authorize home entry. Be aware this is being fought over: a May 2025 federal memo claims ICE can use administrative warrants to enter homes to arrest people with final removal orders, and the government argues for lesser Fourth Amendment protection. Courts have not settled this. The safe course does not change: do not open the door without a judicial warrant.
  • If agents enter anyway, say clearly: 'I do not consent to this entry.' Do not physically resist. Note names, badge numbers, and agency markings, and document everything as soon as it is safe.
  • If you are detained, do not sign anything without a lawyer. Signing a 'voluntary departure' form can have permanent consequences for your ability to stay in or return to the United States.
Say this out loud

“I do not consent to you entering my home.”

“Do you have a judicial warrant signed by a judge? Please slide it under the door.”

“I am exercising my right to remain silent. I want to speak with a lawyer.”

“I do not consent to this entry or search.”

Do

  • Keep the door closed and talk through it.
  • Ask: 'Do you have a judicial warrant signed by a judge?' and have them slide it under the door.
  • Only treat a warrant as valid for home entry if it names a court and is signed by a judge, and it lists your correct name and address.
  • Also check the date on any warrant: a warrant can be expired, and an arrest warrant for someone who does not live at your address does not authorize entry to search for them.
  • Show your ILRC Red Card through the window without opening the door.
  • Know your A-number ahead of time and store it with a trusted person, so family can find you at locator.ice.gov if you are detained.

Do not

  • Do not open the door to receive documents.
  • Do not treat an ICE Form I-200 or I-205 as authority to enter your home.
  • Do not answer questions about your immigration status or birthplace.
  • Do not sign anything, especially a 'voluntary departure' form, without a lawyer.
  • Do not lie or show false documents, which can permanently bar you and bring criminal charges.
Tennessee note

Knox County Sheriff's Office participates in the 287(g) program, so an arrest for ANY charge, even a minor one, can trigger an immigration hold at the Roger D. Wilson Detention Facility. Avoiding situations that lead to arrest is itself protective. A person detained by ICE may take more than 24 hours to appear in the online locator, and children under 18 do not appear there at all.

If You Are Stopped by Police or at a Protest

Stay calm, keep your hands visible, and do not run. Ask whether you are free to go. Invoke your rights out loud, comply physically, and fight an unlawful stop later in court, not on the street.

Your rights

  • You have the right to remain silent. To use it you must say so: 'I am exercising my right to remain silent.' The Supreme Court has held that simply staying quiet is not enough.
  • Tennessee has no traditional stop-and-identify law. In a purely voluntary encounter you generally do not have to give your name, show ID, or answer questions. Ask: 'Am I being detained, or am I free to go?' If free, walk away calmly.
  • Refusing to identify yourself is not by itself a crime in Tennessee. But if you are being cited or arrested for an offense and cannot or will not show satisfactory identification, Tennessee law (T.C.A. 40-7-118) lets an officer take you into custody and book you instead of releasing you with a citation. The rules during a brief investigative detention short of arrest are less settled, so this is fact-specific.
  • If you are driving, you must show your driver's license, vehicle registration, and proof of insurance. Passengers generally do not have to identify themselves. No one has to answer questions about immigration status.
  • Tennessee law does not require an officer to tell you the reason for a stop. A 2025 bill that would have required this never passed. You may calmly ask why you were stopped, but do not demand an answer or refuse to cooperate because none is given.
  • You have the right to refuse consent to a search. Police may still pat down your outer clothing if they reasonably suspect you are armed, and you cannot physically stop that, but say: 'I do not consent to this search.'
  • If you are arrested, say: 'I want a lawyer,' and stop answering questions. In Knox County, ask for the Public Defender if you cannot afford a lawyer.
  • If you do not speak English, ask for an interpreter: 'I need an interpreter.' Courts must provide one, but police on the street may not, so say you want a lawyer, stay silent, and do not sign anything you cannot fully read and understand.
Say this out loud

“Am I being detained, or am I free to go?”

“I am exercising my right to remain silent.”

“I do not consent to this search.”

“I want a lawyer.”

“I need an interpreter.”

Do

  • Stay calm, keep hands visible, and comply physically even if you believe the stop is unlawful.
  • Ask 'Am I being detained, or am I free to go?' and leave calmly if you are free.
  • Write down officer names, badge numbers, patrol car numbers, agency, and witnesses as soon as it is safe.
  • Write a trusted contact or the NLG mass defense number on your arm before any protest, in case your phone is taken.

Do not

  • Do not lie about your name, citizenship, or history. Lying can be a separate crime.
  • Do not run, physically resist, or interfere with an arrest, even of someone else.
  • Do not block a road, street, or sidewalk at a protest without a permit. Obstruction is a crime in Tennessee and rises to a felony if it keeps an emergency vehicle or first responder from getting through or blocks an emergency exit. Blockers can also be sued for money damages by anyone injured.
  • Do not ignore a clear, audible dispersal order. Comply in the moment and challenge it later; refusing a lawful dispersal order is a criminal offense.
Tennessee note

As of May 2026, TIRRC has sued the Tennessee Highway Patrol alleging it stops motorists with ICE agents riding along, then detains people for status checks. That litigation is ongoing, but it means any traffic stop carries heightened risk for people without legal status. Drive carefully, keep documents current, and know your rights before you drive.

Recording the Police in Tennessee

Recording can protect you and your community, but Tennessee added a new limit in 2025 that you must know. You can still record. You just have to keep your distance when ordered.

Your rights

  • Tennessee is a one-party consent state (T.C.A. 39-13-601), so you may record any conversation you are part of.
  • You have a strong First Amendment interest in recording police performing their public duties. Be aware this is not fully settled in Tennessee: the Sixth Circuit, which covers Tennessee, has not issued a published decision squarely establishing a public right to record police. Many other circuits have recognized it, but it is less firmly settled here.
  • Tennessee's PEACE Act took effect July 1, 2025. If an officer performing official duties orders you to keep back, you must stay at least 25 feet away or you can face a Class B misdemeanor. This does not ban recording. You may keep filming from 25 feet or more.
  • Police may never delete your footage. Whether or not you are arrested, an officer generally needs a warrant to look through your phone, though police may sometimes hold the phone itself while they seek one. Do not physically resist, and say clearly that you do not consent to any search.
Say this out loud

“I am recording. I do not consent to you searching or deleting my phone.”

“I am moving back. I am continuing to record from here.”

Do

  • Hold your phone visibly and record from a safe distance.
  • Step back and keep at least 25 feet if an officer orders you to keep your distance, and keep recording from there.
  • Back up your footage as soon as you can.

Do not

  • Do not physically interfere with officers or stand in their path.
  • Do not refuse a lawful order to step back, since that can be charged even if you are not interfering.
  • Do not let an officer delete your footage or search your phone without a warrant. Say you do not consent.
Tennessee note

The PEACE Act's 25-foot buffer has no press exemption and is being challenged in federal court, but as of 2026 it is in effect and enforceable. Assume it applies, keep your distance, and keep recording from a distance. Before any printed run of this guide, confirm the PEACE Act's current status, since the litigation is live.

Your Rights as a Tenant Facing Eviction

Knox County is covered by Tennessee's Uniform Residential Landlord and Tenant Act (URLTA), which applies to counties that had more than 75,000 residents in the 2010 census. That gives Knox tenants real protections. Only a court can evict you, and your landlord must follow specific steps first. Tenants in most smaller, rural East Tennessee counties are not covered by URLTA and rely mostly on their lease and common law, with fewer protections.

Your rights

  • Only a court can evict you. A landlord cannot change your locks, remove your belongings, or shut off your utilities to force you out. This 'self-help' eviction is illegal in URLTA counties like Knox (T.C.A. 66-28-504). In non-URLTA counties the rules are less clear, so call Legal Aid right away if it happens to you.
  • Before filing to evict, a landlord in a URLTA county must generally give you written notice. For nonpayment of rent, a 14-day notice to pay or the lease ends (T.C.A. 66-28-505). Check your lease, though: if it waives this notice in bold print, the landlord can go straight to court with no notice, so never ignore court papers just because no notice came first. For a curable lease violation, a 14-day notice to fix it, or 7 days if the same violation recurs within 6 months.
  • If you live in public housing, Section 8, LIHTC, or a home with a federally backed mortgage, you are entitled to a 30-day notice to vacate before a nonpayment filing, longer than Tennessee's 14 days.
  • Late fees are limited under T.C.A. 66-28-201(d): a landlord cannot charge a late fee until rent is 5 days late, and the fee is capped at 10 percent of the overdue amount.
  • For serious violations, including violence, real threats to health and safety, or an unauthorized subtenant or occupant who refuses to leave, a landlord may terminate on 3 days' written notice (T.C.A. 66-28-517). This ends the tenancy; it is not just a demand to remove the extra person.
  • To end a month-to-month tenancy, either side must give at least 30 days' written notice (T.C.A. 66-28-512).
  • You have the right to a court hearing. Eviction is filed as a 'detainer warrant' in General Sessions Court. If you receive one, show up. If you do not appear, you can lose by default. If you lose, you generally have 10 days to appeal.
  • Appealing an eviction is not free. You must post a bond, and to stay in the home during the appeal you may have to post a possessory bond that can equal a year's rent (T.C.A. 29-18-130(b)(2)). After the 10 days a writ of possession can issue and removal can happen fast.
  • Your landlord must keep your unit fit and habitable and meet housing codes that affect health and safety (T.C.A. 66-28-304). In practice that covers basics like a sound structure, plumbing, water, heat, and electrical, largely through local housing codes. Give written notice of problems and keep a copy. Do not withhold rent or repair-and-deduct on your own without legal advice, because each remedy has exact steps.
  • Tennessee's retaliation protection is narrow (T.C.A. 66-28-514). It covers complaints you make to your landlord and use of your legal remedies. It does not clearly cover reporting code violations to the city or organizing with other tenants. Organizing is lawful and worth doing, but do not assume state law shields you from a nonrenewal or eviction over it; talk to Legal Aid early. And if you are behind on rent, the landlord may proceed for nonpayment regardless.
  • Your landlord cannot keep any of your deposit without an itemized list of damages, and must send notice of any refund due to your last known address (T.C.A. 66-28-301). Tennessee sets no fixed deadline for sending the money, so act fast: give a written forwarding address, and respond within 60 days of the landlord's notice or you forfeit the whole deposit.
  • The landlord can also pursue damages found within 30 days after you move out or 7 days after a new tenant moves in, so photograph the unit at move-out.
  • Your rights under your lease and URLTA apply regardless of immigration status. Tennessee law does not specifically make it illegal for a landlord to threaten to report you to immigration, though in some cases such threats can violate federal fair housing law. Take the threat seriously: document it, do not escalate on your own, and call TIRRC or Legal Aid before your next step.
Say this out loud

“Only a court can evict me. I am not leaving without a court order.”

“I am giving you written notice of this repair. Please fix it within the time the law allows.”

“Here is my forwarding address in writing. Please return my security deposit as the law requires.”

Do

  • Put everything in writing and keep copies. Certified mail is hardest to dispute.
  • Photograph your unit at move-in and move-out, with dates.
  • Show up to your court hearing. If you cannot afford a lawyer, call Legal Aid of East Tennessee before the hearing date.
  • If your landlord locks you out or cuts utilities, do not leave. Call Legal Aid immediately and document it.

Do not

  • Do not assume a verbal lease or no lease means you have no rights. You still have rights, though fewer.
  • Do not withhold rent or repair-and-deduct without legal advice first.
  • Do not let a landlord intimidate you into leaving without a court order.
  • Do not skip giving a written forwarding address, or you may forfeit your deposit.
Tennessee note

The specific day-counts above apply in URLTA counties like Knox. In smaller, non-URLTA East Tennessee counties the rules differ and are governed largely by your lease and common law. Tennessee also has no rent control and bars cities from enacting it, so there is no legal cap on rent increases at the end of a term. If you are unsure whether your county is covered, contact Legal Aid of East Tennessee.

Your Rights as a Worker

You have the right to be paid for your work, to a safe workplace, and to organize with your coworkers. These rights apply regardless of immigration status, and the federal wage agency keeps complaints confidential and does not ask about your status.

Your rights

  • You have the right to be paid at least the federal minimum wage of $7.25 per hour for every hour worked. Tennessee has no higher minimum. Tipped workers must still reach $7.25 per hour once tips are counted, and the employer must make up any shortfall.
  • You have the right to overtime pay at 1.5 times your regular rate for hours over 40 in a week under the Fair Labor Standards Act. Being paid a salary does not by itself take this right away, though some salaried managers and professionals are exempt. If you are salaried and unsure, ask the Department of Labor.
  • These wage rights apply regardless of immigration status. The U.S. Department of Labor Wage and Hour Division enforces them without regard to status, keeps complaints confidential, and can help in more than 200 languages. You generally have up to 2 years to file, or 3 if the violation was willful.
  • You have the right to organize with coworkers, with or without a union. When two or more workers act together on a workplace problem, that is protected concerted activity under the National Labor Relations Act. Your employer cannot legally fire, demote, cut hours, or threaten you for it. You have 6 months from the retaliation to file a charge with the NLRB.
  • You can discuss your pay with coworkers. An employer policy forbidding it is almost always illegal.
  • Undocumented workers are covered by the NLRA and may organize, though certain remedies like back pay can be limited by a 2002 Supreme Court ruling (Hoffman Plastic). The NLRA does not cover some workers, including most government, agricultural, and domestic workers, independent contractors, and supervisors.
  • You have the right to a safe workplace under OSHA and Tennessee's TOSHA. You have a narrow right to refuse work that poses a real risk of death or serious harm when the employer will not fix it and there is no time to call OSHA. Retaliation complaints to OSHA must be filed within 30 days.
  • Tennessee is an at-will state, but your employer cannot fire you for an illegal reason. Tennessee's Public Protection Act (T.C.A. 50-1-304) protects employees, public and private, who refuse to break the law or refuse to stay silent about illegal activity; you have 1 year to sue. Federal law also bars firing you for filing a wage or safety complaint or for organizing.
  • Undocumented workers in Tennessee are covered by workers' compensation, including medical care and the basic disability award, though state law denies certain increased benefits to workers not authorized to work. File anyway, and get advice on what you are owed. In a 2026 Tennessee case, a workers' compensation judge ordered an employer to keep providing medical care to a worker even after his deportation, though that ruling is an early-stage order, not a final decision.
Say this out loud

“My right to discuss wages with coworkers is protected under federal law.”

“I want to confirm in writing that on this date you took this action after I raised this concern.”

“I am reporting this confidentially. Please do not disclose my immigration status.”

Do

  • Write down dates, hours, and amounts owed, and keep pay stubs and schedules.
  • File a wage complaint first, before telling your employer, then document any change in how you are treated.
  • Save texts, emails, witness names, and dates. Under new NLRB intake procedures, you must submit supporting evidence within two weeks of filing a charge, or the charge can be dismissed.
  • If you are undocumented and unsure, call TIRRC, Legal Aid, or Catholic Charities before filing anything in writing, since the labor-dispute immigration shield (DALE) ended in early 2025.

Do not

  • Do not wait to file. Evidence and deadlines run out.
  • Do not accept an employer's claim that you cannot discuss wages. That policy is almost always illegal.
  • Do not let a workers' comp claim be denied because of your status. Tennessee covers you.
  • Do not assume you need a union to be protected. Two workers acting together can be enough.
Tennessee note

File federal wage claims with the U.S. Department of Labor at 1-866-487-9243. File Tennessee safety complaints with TOSHA at (800) 249-8510. For organizing retaliation, file with the NLRB at no cost. Immigration status is not asked in a federal wage investigation.

How to Plug Into Community Defense With the Chapter

Rights on paper matter more when we back each other up. Knoxville DSA organizes so that no neighbor faces ICE, an eviction, or wage theft alone. Here is how to get involved and how to prepare your household before an emergency.

Your rights

  • You have the right to prepare. Making a family preparedness plan, printing Red Cards, and knowing the phone numbers is not paranoia. It is how a stressful moment stays survivable.
  • You have the right to organize with your neighbors, tenants, and coworkers. Federal labor law protects most workers acting together, Tennessee law protects tenant organizing against landlord retaliation in Knox County, and the First Amendment protects peaceful assembly. Collective action is the most powerful tool any of us has.
  • You have the right to ask for help. The organizations on this page exist to be called. Reaching out early is always better than waiting.
Say this out loud

“I want to help build community defense in Knoxville. How do I get trained?”

“Here is the number for legal help. You are not alone in this.”

Do

  • Make a family preparedness plan now. Designate a trusted caregiver for your children in writing, gather key documents, and decide what your children should do if you are detained. The ILRC has a free step-by-step plan.
  • Print ILRC Red Cards in the languages your household uses and keep one in your wallet. Print the wallet card on this page too.
  • Save the numbers in your phone and on paper someone else can reach: your attorney, the TIRRC Resource Line, and a trusted contact.
  • Come to a chapter meeting or a Know Your Rights training. Ask about the community defense working group.
  • Volunteer as a trained legal observer, help staff a phone tree, or help translate and distribute these materials.
  • If you witness an ICE operation, connect people to legal help through the TIRRC Resource Line, document what you can from a safe distance, and obey any order to keep back at least 25 feet while you keep recording.
  • Connect with Allies of Knoxville's Immigrant Neighbors (weareakin.wordpress.com), the closest thing to a local rapid-response network. It is not a bond fund and has no verified hotline, so confirm its current activity and contact before relying on it.
  • Remember that the NLG Federal Defense Hotline and the U.S. Department of Labor wage line are business-hours lines, not 24/7 emergency dispatch. The Department of Labor line runs Monday to Friday, roughly 8:00 to 4:30 local time, so plan around their hours and confirm the TIRRC line's hours when you call.

Do not

  • Do not wait until an emergency to learn the numbers or make a plan.
  • Do not try to physically intervene in an arrest. Observe, document, and connect people to legal help.
  • Do not spread unverified rumors of ICE activity. Confirm through trusted networks before sharing.
Tennessee note

We could not verify a Knoxville-specific or Tennessee-specific immigrant bond or deportation defense fund. Route bond and defense-fund questions through the TIRRC Resource Line at (615) 414-1030, and re-verify any rapid-response reporting line before relying on it, because those numbers change often.

TIRRC (Tennessee Immigrant and Refugee Rights Coalition) Resource Line

Statewide immigrant and refugee-led coalition. Its Resource Line gives information and referrals on housing, employment, school enrollment, public benefits such as WIC and SNAP, and naturalization. It is also a reasonable first call for other immigration questions, including ICE encounters and bonds, but confirm what the line can help with when you call and ask for a referral if it cannot help directly. ACLU of Tennessee

Statewide civil liberties organization. Publishes Know Your Rights materials on police, ICE, and worker rights, and litigates civil rights and immigration enforcement cases. It does not handle individual landlord-tenant or wage cases. Legal intake is by online form. Operates statewide from Nashville with no Knoxville walk-in office.

(615) 320-7142; legal intake at aclu-tn.org/request-legal-assistance

(212) 679-2811; massdef@nlg.org (Mass Defense Program, not the hotline)

1-866-487-9243 (1-866-4USWAGE)

ILRC Red Card and Family Preparedness Plan

The Immigrant Legal Resource Center publishes the free Red Card (a wallet-sized card in many languages that lets you assert your rights without speaking) and a free step-by-step family preparedness plan. Print both before you need them.

Download free at ilrc.org/red-cards and ilrc.org/community-resources/family-preparedness-plan

ICE Online Detainee Locator

Official government tool to find a person detained by ICE, by A-number and country of birth, or by name and country of birth. It can take more than 24 hours for a detained person to appear. Children under 18 do not appear in the system.
