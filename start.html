You belong here

Most people who walk into their first Knoxville DSA meeting are nervous about something. You are not the exception, and whatever is holding you back, you are in good company. Here are the worries we hear the most, and the honest answer to each.

“I have never organized anything”

Good. Neither had most of us. You do not need a background in politics, a degree, or a plan. You need to care about your neighbors, and you clearly already do, or you would not be reading this. We will show you the ropes one step at a time.

“I am not sure I know enough about socialism”

You do not have to pass a quiz at the door. Plenty of members joined curious and learned as they went. If you want to dig in, the chapter runs the Little Red Schoolhouse, our political education group, and it is built for exactly this. Until then, “I want my city to work for working people” is more than enough.

“I am nervous to walk in alone”

That feeling is the most normal thing in the world, and we plan for it. Newcomers are greeted on purpose, in the room and in the Zoom chat, so you are never left standing by yourself. You can also bring a friend, and a lot of people do.

“I am short on time”

Then start small. Coming to one meeting costs you an evening and zero dollars. From there you decide how much you take on, and it is genuinely fine to do a little. Many members give an hour here and there, and that adds up across a whole chapter.

What actually happens at a meeting

A General Meeting is where the whole chapter comes together to decide things, hear updates from the working groups, and plan what comes next. It usually opens with a welcome and introductions, moves through a short agenda shared ahead of time, and wraps with clear ways to plug in if you want them.

You will not be put on the spot. You can listen the whole time, you can ask a question in the chat, or you can jump into the conversation, whatever feels right. Decisions are made together by the members in the room, which is exactly why your voice matters once you join.

Meetings run about an hour to ninety minutes. Come a few minutes early if you can, say hello, and you will get the lay of the land fast.

How to get in the door

Meetings are hybrid: join online over Zoom, or come to the in-person spot we announce with the agenda. RSVP through our Action Network page and the Zoom link comes to you, so you always know exactly where to go.

First time? Greeters look after newcomers on purpose, in the room and in the chat. You will not be left to figure it out alone.

Honest answers

The questions newcomers ask

If you are wondering it, someone else wondered it first. Here are the ones we hear before that first meeting.

Do I have to be a member to come?

No. General Meetings and most working groups are open to everyone, members or not. Come, look around, and see if it is for you before you decide anything.

What does it cost to show up?

Nothing. Coming to a meeting is free, and there are no dues to attend. If you later choose to join DSA, dues are sliding scale and start where you can afford them, with waivers available, no questions asked.

Do I need to know a lot about socialism?

Not at all. Curiosity is the only prerequisite. If you want to learn more, our political education group, the Little Red Schoolhouse, is open to members and newcomers alike. Take a look at Political Education whenever you are ready.

Is everything online?

Our General Meetings are hybrid. You can join over Zoom from home, or come to the in-person spot we announce with each agenda. Working groups vary, and each group’s page tells you how it meets.

I am nervous about showing up alone. What then?

That is the most common worry of all, and we plan around it. Newcomers are greeted on purpose, in the room and in the chat, so you are never stranded. You will have someone to talk to within the first few minutes.

How much time does this take?

As much or as little as you have. A meeting is one evening. Beyond that, you set your own pace, and giving an hour here and there is genuinely useful. Nobody is keeping score.

Can I bring a friend?

Please do. Bringing someone along is one of the easiest ways to feel comfortable walking in, and friends are always welcome at open meetings and most working groups.
