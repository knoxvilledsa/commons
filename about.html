About

Who we are

“We believe that both the economy and society should be run democratically to meet human needs, not to make profits for a few.”

A chapter of your neighbors

Knoxville Area DSA is the local chapter of the Democratic Socialists of America, the largest socialist organization in the country. DSA is not a political party. It is a member-run organization of working people who believe ordinary people, not a wealthy few, should decide how our economy and our communities are run.

We organize here because East Tennessee deserves better than what the market alone will give it. Rents climb while wages stall, the bus comes too rarely to count on, and the people who keep this place running are the last ones consulted about its future. A democratic-socialist chapter in Knoxville is how your neighbors push back together, in public, on the things that shape daily life.

Our work runs through working groups: public transportation, housing and tenant power, labor, eco-socialism, political education, electoral politics, mutual aid, community defense, and queer liberation. Each one is an open door. You do not need experience or a membership card to walk in. See what we organize for and find your group.

A chapter rebuilt by its members

We will tell you plainly where the chapter has been, because how we got here is part of the case for joining. In 2025 the chapter was administratively dissolved. Rather than let that be the end, members chose to rebuild it from the ground up, through open and democratic work that anyone can see and take part in.

That rebuilding is happening right now. Members ran a full revision of the chapter bylaws, and that revision is heading to two member votes, on Monday, August 24 and Monday, September 28, 2026. Nothing about it is settled behind closed doors. The proposed text, the existing bylaws, and the rest of our governing documents are all public, and the decision belongs to the members in the room.

We treat this as a strength, not something to tuck away. A chapter that is being rebuilt by its own members in the open is a chapter where your voice actually counts and where you can help shape what comes next. Read the documents for yourself in Documents, or follow the rebuild as it happens in News.

How the chapter runs

Decisions are made by members at the monthly General Meeting, where proposals are debated and voted on in the open. Between meetings, an elected Steering Committee keeps the day-to-day work moving, and the working groups carry the campaigns. A four-tier hierarchy of documents, Bylaws then Codes then Rules then Policies, keeps it all accountable and predictable, so that members, not officers, hold the final say.

That is the short version. For the committees, the working-group structure, and how a proposal becomes chapter policy, see the full breakdown.

Leadership

The chapter runs on roles, not personalities. Here are the offices that keep it accountable to its members.

Officers

Co-Chairs

Steer the chapter, set the direction with the membership, and run the General Meetings.

Officer

Secretary

Keeps the minutes and governance records, sends notice of meetings, and maintains the registries of codes, rules, and policies.

Officer

Treasurer

Handles the chapter’s finances and keeps it in good standing with its compliance obligations.

Officer

Steward

The chapter’s lead internal organizing officer: new-member onboarding and retention, the membership roster, and the count that sets quorum. Named for the shop-steward tradition.

Independent office

Harassment Grievance Officer

An elected office that handles grievances independently, so concerns are heard fairly and apart from the rest of leadership.

The two Co-Chairs, the Secretary, the Treasurer, and the Steward together make up the Steering Committee, the chapter’s board that carries out decisions between General Meetings. Every officer is elected by the members, and there is no public officer roster here by design. To reach the current officers, use Contact and your message will be routed to the right person.

Community Agreements

The chapter holds a set of Community Agreements that set the tone for how members treat one another. The summary below captures the spirit of them. It is not the official text.

  • Comradely conduct: we argue about ideas without tearing down the people in the room.
  • Accountability: we own our impact, repair harm, and follow through on what we commit to.
  • Anti-oppression and anti-harassment: racism, sexism, homophobia, transphobia, and harassment have no home here.
  • Confidentiality where it is needed, so people can speak and seek help safely.
  • Good-faith participation: we show up honestly, share the work, and assume the best of each other.

This is a plain-language summary. The full Community Agreements, along with the Code of Conduct and the Harassment and Grievance Policy, live in Documents.
